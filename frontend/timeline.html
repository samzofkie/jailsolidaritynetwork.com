past and present in cook county jail

All historical research is from Melanie Newports This Is My Jail.

All 2023 testimony was collected from insiders by the Jail Solidarity Network.

1929

The new jail opens next to the House of Correction, excising Black people from Chicago's downtown and "to a neighborhood of stockyards, factories, and railroads" (37). Throughout the 1920s, prisoners were making 2.3 million bricks per year, which they then used to build a new wall around the jail incarcerating them.

1950

A federal grand jury charged Cook County Jail as "rotten from top to bottom: "One warden admitted it was 'no secret' that guards facilitated a booming trade in narcotics, alcohol, razor blades, hypodermic needles, and knives" (47).

Present

In 2023, as nearly 20 men died by overdose in the jail, administrators blamed these deaths on visitors, volunteers, and lawyers smuggling in drugs via paper; this suspicion also led to them banning paper from entering, a rule still in place. National evidence indicates that the vast majority of substances used inside are carried in by correctional staffthe only people charged in 2023 were a nurse and a commissary worker employed in the jail.

1954

Present

People incarcerated in the jail protested conditions and were brutalized with tear gas and high pressure hoses. Other protestsincluding a food fight and hunger strikearose over poor food quality (45). This was only one of hundreds of inside protests at all scales, often erased in a history of reform at Cook County Jail.

Adding to the aggression is the sparse amount of food that is distributed, because I mean, I've lost weight, and I already went in there weighing I think I weighed in at like 148 or something like that. I can only imagine I'm probably like, down to the 130s by now. And that's only after like three weeks (Anon).

1955

Sheriff Lohman, elected in 1954, heavily publicized his desire to reform Cook County Jail through cheap and incremental changes such as creating programming to occupy prisoners and provide outlets for discontent, and building out new spaces to serve prisoners, such as recreation areas, a chapel, and a hospital area (58, 57). 

Present

Today, Sheriff Dart boasts many of these small changessuch as volunteer programming, rec areas, and expanded substance use services. Yet only a fraction of insiders can access programming (which is only available on certain tiers in two of the divisions), many experience medical neglect and wait months in between outdoor time.
